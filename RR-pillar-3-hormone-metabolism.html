Metabolism.

How estrogen is processed is as important as how much you have.

Most hormone conversations stop at production — how much estrogen, whether your levels are “normal.” What no one asks is what happens to estrogen after it does its job. Where it goes, how it’s broken down, and whether your liver, your gut, and your genes are handling that breakdown in a way that protects you or harms you. That question changes everything.

The Liver Process

Estrogen metabolism is
a two-step process.

Most women — and most clinicians — think of estrogen as a hormone you either have enough of or don’t. But estrogen must be metabolized and eliminated after it acts. That process requires two distinct liver phases, and failing either one produces toxic metabolites that drive breast tenderness, fibrocystic changes, mood instability, and increased cancer risk.

Phase 1 — Hydroxylation

The liver converts estradiol (E2) into one of three estrogen metabolites, each with a different biological activity and risk profile. The ratio of these metabolites is measurable on the DUTCH panel — and is one of the most clinically actionable findings available.

2-OHE1
2-Hydroxyestrone
The Protective Metabolite
Weakly estrogenic and anti-proliferative — inhibits estrogen-sensitive cell growth. Women with higher 2-OHE1 production have lower breast cancer risk. The metabolite you want dominant. Supported by cruciferous vegetables, adequate methylation, and anti-inflammatory lifestyle.
Protective — aim for dominance · 2:16 target above 2.0
4-OHE1
4-Hydroxyestrone
The Genotoxic Metabolite
Forms DNA-reactive quinones that directly damage genetic material. Requires COMT-mediated methylation for neutralization. Elevated 4-OHE1 with slow COMT = the highest-risk combination measurable on DUTCH. Elevated by alcohol, inflammation, PPIs, and certain plastics.
Genotoxic — minimize and support clearance via COMT methylation
16α-OHE1
16α-Hydroxyestrone
The Proliferative Metabolite
Strongly estrogenic and pro-proliferative. Drives breast tenderness, fibrocystic changes, fibroids, and endometriosis progression. The 2:16 ratio (target above 2.0) is a primary cancer risk marker. Elevated by obesity, alcohol, hypothyroidism, and omega-6 excess.
Proliferative — monitor 2:16 ratio on DUTCH · DIM and weight management
Phase 2 — Conjugation — Where Most Women Silently Fail

Phase 1 metabolites must be conjugated — chemically neutralized and packaged for excretion. This step requires specific nutrient cofactors. When Phase 2 is impaired, Phase 1 metabolites accumulate and recirculate.

Methylation (COMT-dependent)
The primary Phase 2 pathway for 4-OHE1. COMT methylates and neutralizes catechol estrogens, preventing quinone formation. Requires SAMe, magnesium, and folate. Slow COMT variants dramatically impair this pathway.
Glucuronidation
Primary pathway for packaging estrogen for bile and stool excretion. This is the pathway the estrobolome (Section 02) can undo — gut bacteria with high beta-glucuronidase activity cleave the bond and release estrogen back into circulation.
Sulfation
Secondary conjugation pathway — vulnerable to disruption by xenoestrogens (BPA, phthalates), NSAIDs, and dietary factors. Impaired sulfation shifts more estrogen to glucuronidation, increasing the burden on an already-taxed system.
The Clinical Bottom Line

A woman can have “normal” estrogen on a serum panel and be metabolizing it through the 4-OHE1 pathway — silently accumulating genotoxic quinones that no standard blood test would ever reveal. This is why a DUTCH metabolite panel is not optional. It is the test that changes the conversation from “your levels are fine” to “here’s exactly what your body is doing with the estrogen it has.”

The Gut-Hormone Connection

Your gut bacteria control
your estrogen levels.

The estrobolome is the collection of gut bacteria that metabolize estrogen. When healthy, it ensures proper estrogen elimination. When dysbiotic, it recirculates estrogen that was supposed to leave your body — creating a form of estrogen excess that no hormone prescription will fix.

01
Liver conjugates estrogen — Phase 1 and Phase 2 processing packages estrogen metabolites with glucuronic acid for bile excretion.
02
Conjugated estrogen enters the gut — glucuronidated metabolites travel through bile into the small intestine and colon, ready for elimination.
03
Beta-glucuronidase activity — gut bacteria produce this enzyme which can cleave the glucuronic acid bond, releasing active estrogen back into circulation.
04
Reabsorption or excretion — if beta-glucuronidase is HIGH, estrogen reabsorbs. If LOW and microbiome is balanced, estrogen exits in stool.
05
Clinical result — high activity = elevated circulating estrogen from reabsorption, invisible on standard labs. You end up with more estrogen than your ovaries or HRT produced.
The Constipation Connection

Slow transit dramatically increases estrogen reabsorption. Constipation is not a minor discomfort — it is a significant hormonal risk factor. Daily bowel movements are a clinical target. This is why the gut protocol in Section 07 includes specific bowel motility support.

The HRT Complication

Women on HRT with high beta-glucuronidase accumulate more estrogen than their dose should produce — explaining unexpected side effects at low doses, breast tenderness, and bloating that practitioners misread as the wrong dose rather than a gut reabsorption problem.

Healthy Estrobolome
Diverse microbiome — 500+ bacterial species in balance
Low beta-glucuronidase — estrogen exits in stool
Daily bowel movements — transit time under 24–36 hours
Butyrate-producing bacteria protecting gut lining
Lactobacillaceae species present and dominant
Circulating estrogen matches what ovaries and HRT produce
Dysbiotic Estrobolome
Low diversity — dominant pathogenic or opportunistic species
High beta-glucuronidase — estrogen deconjugated and reabsorbed
Constipation or slow transit — estrogen contact time extended
Intestinal permeability driving systemic inflammation
SIBO or fungal overgrowth competing with healthy estrobolome species
Circulating estrogen elevated beyond what any lab would explain
Genetic Overlay

COMT — why two women on the
same HRT respond differently.

Catechol-O-methyltransferase is the enzyme responsible for methylating and neutralizing catechol estrogens — particularly the genotoxic 4-OHE1 metabolite. Your COMT variant determines how well your body clears these metabolites, your sensitivity to estrogen on HRT, and your risk profile.

Fast COMT (Val/Val)
The Efficient Metabolizer
Clears catechol estrogens and dopamine rapidly
Generally tolerates estrogen well — may need higher HRT doses for effect
Lower anxiety and higher stress resilience
Less breast tenderness history
Higher caffeine tolerance
May under-respond to supplements at standard doses
DIM at full dose 200–400mg · Standard methylation support · Monitor 2:16 ratio and 4-OHE1 on DUTCH · Less restrictive lifestyle approach
Slow COMT (Met/Met)
The Sensitive Metabolizer
Clears catechol estrogens slowly — 4-OHE1 accumulates
Strong HRT side effects at standard doses — breast tenderness, density increase
History of fibrocystic breast changes
Anxiety, rumination, difficulty “letting go”
High caffeine sensitivity · Pain sensitivity
Mood worse with higher estrogen doses
Aggressive methylation support · Calcium D-glucarate non-optional · DIM at 100–150mg max · Lower HRT dosing with metabolite tracking · Limit alcohol and PPIs
Heterozygous (Val/Met)
The Context-Dependent Metabolizer
Intermediate clearance — ~50% of the population
Variable response — shifts toward slow phenotype under stress
Intermediate estrogen sensitivity
Symptoms worsen with alcohol, poor sleep, high inflammation
Cyclical breast tenderness around HRT changes
Nutrient status strongly influences phenotype expression
Cofactors first (magnesium, B12, folate) · DIM at 200mg · DUTCH repeat at 90 days · Lifestyle factors have outsized impact in this genotype
The Dose Escalation Trap

Slow COMT women on standard HRT doses are not metabolizing catechol estrogens efficiently — 4-OHE1 quinones accumulate that Phase 2 cannot clear fast enough. The standard clinical response of increasing the dose worsens the problem. These women are not treatment-resistant. They are hormonally optimized into a broken cortisol and methylation environment.

Methylation Genetics

MTHFR — the methylation gene
that affects everything.

Methylation is a fundamental biochemical process running over 200 reactions in the body — including Phase 2 estrogen detoxification, neurotransmitter synthesis, DNA repair, and immune function. MTHFR variants impair the conversion of dietary folate into the active form the body needs. In perimenopausal women, this has direct clinical consequences for HRT safety and efficacy.

MTHFR C677T
The Thermolabile Variant
The most studied MTHFR variant. Heterozygous (CT) reduces MTHFR efficiency by approximately 40%. Homozygous (TT) reduces efficiency by approximately 70%. This directly reduces active folate availability, raises homocysteine, and reduces SAMe production — impairing COMT’s ability to clear estrogen metabolites and increasing cardiovascular risk on estrogen HRT.
HRT implication: Oral estrogen dramatically increases clotting risk in C677T women with elevated homocysteine. Transdermal estrogen is strongly preferred. Always check homocysteine before initiating HRT. Target below 8 μmol/L. Protocol: Methylfolate (L-5-MTHF — not folic acid), methyl-B12, riboflavin (B2 is a direct MTHFR cofactor), TMG.
MTHFR A1298C
The BH4 Variant
A1298C primarily affects the BH4 (tetrahydrobiopterin) pathway — a cofactor required for serotonin, dopamine, and nitric oxide synthesis. Particularly relevant for mood symptoms, anxiety, and vascular reactivity. Combined with C677T (compound heterozygous), methylation impairment is significantly more severe than either alone.
HRT implication: A1298C women may experience more pronounced mood effects from estrogen — both positive and negative. Neurotransmitter support is critical alongside hormone management. Protocol: Same as C677T plus BH4 support — L-tyrosine for dopamine precursors, 5-HTP for serotonin support, magnesium. Compound heterozygous: treat as functionally homozygous.
The Folic Acid Problem — Never Folic Acid for MTHFR Variants

Folic acid requires the same enzyme you can’t use. For MTHFR-variant women, folic acid in standard prenatal vitamins and supplements cannot be efficiently converted — and at high doses, unmetabolized folic acid actively competes with 5-MTHF at folate receptors, worsening the deficiency it was meant to correct. L-5-MTHF (Quatrefolic or Metafolin) is the only appropriate form.

The Overlooked Overlay

POTS, MCAS & histamine —
the hormone connection no one makes.

There is a subset of perimenopausal women who do not respond to conventional approaches because they have an underlying dysautonomia, mast cell activation, or histamine intolerance that is being dramatically amplified by the hormonal transition. These conditions are consistently missed, consistently blamed on anxiety or hypochondria, and consistently treatable once identified.

POTS
Postural Orthostatic Tachycardia Syndrome
Autonomic nervous system dysfunction characterized by an abnormal heart rate increase on standing (30+ bpm). 80% female, significantly worsened by perimenopausal estrogen fluctuation. Routinely dismissed as anxiety or deconditioning. Many women are first diagnosed at 40s — not because they developed it then, but because estrogen’s floor dropped out from under a compensating system.
Heart racing or pounding on standing
Lightheadedness, near-fainting on position change
Brain fog described as “head full of cotton”
Exercise intolerance disproportionate to fitness
GI dysmotility — nausea, bloating, constipation
Anxiety worse in the morning or when upright
HRT: Transdermal estrogen supports blood volume and autonomic tone. Progesterone carefully — start low. Coordinate with POTS-aware clinician. Salt + fluid loading (3–5g sodium + 2–3L water daily).
MCAS
Mast Cell Activation Syndrome
Pathological mast cell activation releasing histamine, prostaglandins, and inflammatory mediators inappropriately. Estimated 14–17% of the population, dramatically underdiagnosed. Estrogen directly stimulates mast cell degranulation — estrogen fluctuation in perimenopause destabilizes an already hypersensitive mast cell population.
Flushing, hives, or skin reactions without clear trigger
GI symptoms after varied foods
Reactions to medications, supplements, fragrances
Racing heart, blood pressure drops
Symptoms consistently worse pre-menstrually
Multiple chemical sensitivities
HRT: Bioidentical micronized progesterone far better tolerated than synthetic progestins — stabilizes mast cells. Stabilize mast cells before initiating or adjusting HRT. Transdermal estrogen preferred.
Histamine Intolerance
DAO Enzyme Deficiency
A deficiency of diamine oxidase (DAO) that normally breaks down dietary histamine in the gut. Estrogen inhibits DAO activity while increasing histamine release from mast cells — a self-amplifying loop that perimenopause dramatically worsens. Women cycle between “fine” and “completely falling apart” with food, wine, and stress as triggers.
Headaches after red wine, aged cheese, fermented foods
Flushing of face or neck after eating
GI cramping, diarrhea — food-dependent
Racing heart or palpitations after meals
Symptoms dramatically worse pre-menstrually
HRT: Progesterone raises DAO activity — one of the most underappreciated benefits of progesterone restoration. Introduce estrogen gradually. Low-histamine trial (4–8 weeks) is diagnostic as much as therapeutic.
The POTS-MCAS-Histamine Triad

These three are not separate conditions. They are frequently comorbid — often with hypermobile Ehlers-Danlos Syndrome (hEDS) as an underlying connective tissue component. Sequencing matters: stabilize mast cells first, then address POTS, then introduce hormonal support starting with progesterone, then estrogen last at lowest effective dose with mast cell stabilizers in place.

Testing Strategy

An honest testing roadmap —
where to start and where to go.

The ideal testing picture for Pillar 3 spans three categories. Here is a transparent map of what each test offers and how to sequence them — starting with the single most important test and building from there.

DUTCH
2-OHE1, 4-OHE1, 16α-OHE1 Ratios
2:16 ratio >2.0 · 4-OHE1 as low as possible · Order DUTCH Complete — not the basic panel
The core Phase 1 metabolite panel. Reveals which estrogen pathway is dominant and your genotoxic and proliferative risk profile. The 2:16 ratio and elevated 4-OHE1 are the two most clinically actionable findings. No standard blood draw gives you this information.
Start here. This is the anchor test for Pillar 3. Everything else builds from what this reveals.
DUTCH
2-Methoxyestrone (2-MeOE1)
Low 2-MeOE1 with adequate 2-OHE1 = methylation gap · Indicates COMT impairment
The methylation marker on the DUTCH panel. 2-MeOE1 is the product of COMT methylating 2-OHE1. If 2-OHE1 is present but 2-MeOE1 is low, Phase 1 is working but Phase 2 methylation is impaired — pointing directly to slow COMT activity, MTHFR variants, or nutrient cofactor deficiency.
This single finding makes DIM, methylfolate, and B12 non-optional additions to the protocol.
Serum
Homocysteine (Fasting)
Under 8 μmol/L · Standard lab normal (<15) is inadequate
The most accessible methylation marker in standard serum testing. Elevated homocysteine directly indicates impaired methylation cycle function — from MTHFR variants, B vitamin deficiency, or both. Critical before initiating oral estrogen — high homocysteine combined with oral estrogen dramatically increases clotting risk.
Always check before prescribing HRT. Transdermal route preferred if elevated.
Genetic
MTHFR Genotyping (C677T + A1298C)
One-time panel · Permanent results · Available via LabCorp, Quest, or 23andMe
One-time genetic panel. Indicated for any woman with elevated homocysteine, miscarriage history, clotting events, or strong mood component to her symptoms. Changes the supplement from folic acid to methylfolate and informs HRT route preference.
Done once. Informs lifetime hormone management approach.
Genetic
COMT Genotyping (Val158Met)
Val/Val = fast · Val/Met = intermediate · Met/Met = slow · One-time draw
Identifies fast, slow, or heterozygous COMT status. Changes the entire HRT dosing approach, supplement protocol, and risk assessment. Slow COMT women need lower estrogen doses, aggressive methylation support, and Calcium D-glucarate as standard of care.
Available via pharmacogenomics panels (Genomind) or through 23andMe raw data interpretation.
Stool
GI-MAP or Comprehensive Stool Panel
Beta-glucuronidase activity · Microbiome diversity · Calprotectin · Pathogenic load
Directly measures beta-glucuronidase enzyme activity, microbiome diversity, and intestinal permeability markers. The definitive estrobolome assessment. Clinically indicated when DUTCH shows favorable metabolite ratios but estrogen-dominant symptoms persist, or when significant antibiotic history or GI symptoms are present.
High beta-glucuronidase + favorable DUTCH = gut reabsorption is the driver of the estrogen burden.

Your Action Plan

The Pillar 3
Metabolism Protocol.

This protocol supports all three metabolism pathways simultaneously — liver Phase 1 and 2, estrobolome, and methylation — while acknowledging that COMT and MTHFR genetic status should personalize the approach. Always open Phase 2 support before or simultaneously with Phase 1 inducers like DIM.

Liver Support
Phase 1 and Phase 2 Optimization
DIM (Diindolylmethane)
100–400mg · Dose by COMT status · BioResponse DIM preferred
Shifts Phase 1 toward protective 2-OHE1 pathway. Slow COMT: 100–150mg max — higher doses increase catechol estrogen load beyond what slow COMT can methylate. Fast COMT: 200–400mg. Take with fat for absorption. Open Phase 2 support first.
With largest fatty meal · Open methylation support first
Calcium D-Glucarate
500–1,000mg daily in divided doses
Directly inhibits beta-glucuronidase — essential for women with estrogen-dominant symptoms, fibroids, or history of high 16α-OHE1 on DUTCH. Non-optional for slow COMT women — reduces the 4-OHE1 burden that slow COMT struggles to clear. Also supports Phase 2 glucuronidation capacity in the liver.
500mg AM · 500mg PM with meals
NAC (N-Acetyl Cysteine)
600–1,200mg daily
Glutathione precursor — replenishes the liver’s master antioxidant depleted by catechol estrogen quinone formation. Particularly important in slow COMT women where 4-OHE1 quinones accumulate. Also supports sulfation Phase 2 pathway directly.
Away from meals for best absorption
Can worsen symptoms in MCAS — start at 200mg and titrate slowly
Sulforaphane
30–60mg standardized broccoli sprout extract
Activates the NRF2 pathway — the master regulator of cellular antioxidant defense. Induces Phase 2 liver enzymes including glutathione S-transferase and glucuronosyltransferase. Works synergistically with DIM. Emerging evidence for direct breast cancer risk reduction.
Morning · Allow 45 min before coffee — coffee reduces NRF2 activation
SAMe
400–800mg on empty stomach
The universal methyl donor — directly fuels COMT-mediated methylation of catechol estrogens. Particularly indicated for slow COMT and MTHFR variants where endogenous SAMe production is impaired. Works synergistically with methylfolate and B12.
Empty stomach AM · Can cause insomnia if taken late
Avoid in bipolar disorder — can trigger hypomania
Milk Thistle (Silymarin)
140–420mg standardized silymarin
Hepatoprotective — protects liver cells from Phase 1 oxidative stress and supports Phase 2 enzyme activity. Particularly important for women with alcohol history, high medication burden, or elevated liver enzymes.
With meals · Can be taken alongside DIM
Gut & Estrobolome
Restoring the Gut-Hormone Pathway
Spore-Based Probiotic
Megasporebiotic · 2 caps daily · 8-week minimum
Spore-forming Bacillus strains (B. indicus, B. subtilis, B. coagulans) survive stomach acid and profoundly shift microbiome composition toward diversity. Preferred over standard Lactobacillus in women with suspected SIBO or previous antibiotic courses. Builds the diverse microbiome that keeps beta-glucuronidase activity in check.
With meals · 8-week minimum before evaluating
Ground Flaxseed
2 tablespoons daily · Freshly ground · Store frozen
The single most underused hormonal food intervention. Contains lignans that bind estrogen in the gut and reduce reabsorption, insoluble fiber that feeds the estrobolome, and omega-3s that reduce inflammation. Must be ground — whole seeds pass undigested. Effect on estrogen metabolism measurable within 6–8 weeks.
Add to morning smoothie, yogurt, or oatmeal daily
Magnesium Glycinate (Bowel Motility)
300–600mg evening
Daily bowel movements reduce estrogen contact time with gut bacteria and minimize reabsorption. Magnesium glycinate at higher evening doses supports bowel motility and sleep simultaneously — connecting Pillar 1, Pillar 2, and Pillar 3 in a single intervention.
Evening · Bowel regularity is the clinical target
L-Glutamine + Zinc Carnosine
L-Glutamine 5g + Zinc Carnosine 37.5mg
Intestinal permeability allows bacterial LPS into circulation — driving systemic inflammation that activates the HPA axis (Pillar 2) and impairs liver detoxification. L-glutamine is the primary fuel source for intestinal epithelial cells. Zinc carnosine has specific evidence for mucosal healing.
L-Glutamine on empty stomach AM/PM · Zinc Carnosine with meals
Methylation
Supporting the Methylation Cycle — Sequence Matters
Start Low — Titrate Slowly

Support methylation cofactors (B12, B6, riboflavin, magnesium) before adding methylfolate. Start methylfolate at 400mcg and increase slowly — overmethylation can cause anxiety, insomnia, and irritability in sensitive individuals, particularly slow COMT women.

L-5-Methyltetrahydrofolate
400mcg–1mg · Quatrefolic or Metafolin · Never folic acid
Active folate that bypasses MTHFR entirely. Essential for MTHFR variants. Start at 400mcg and build to 800mcg–1mg over 4–6 weeks. For compound heterozygous women, consider 1mg with clinical supervision.
AM with breakfast
Methylcobalamin (B12)
1,000–2,000mcg sublingual
Methyl-B12 partners with methylfolate in the methylation cycle. Sublingual bypasses gut conversion — essential for women with history of PPIs, low stomach acid, or gut dysbiosis. For slow COMT women who react poorly, hydroxocobalamin is better tolerated.
AM sublingual · Hold under tongue 30 seconds
Riboflavin (B2)
100–400mg daily
Direct MTHFR enzyme cofactor — included in few standard B-complexes at therapeutic doses. B2 supplementation can normalize homocysteine in C677T homozygous individuals even when folate is already adequate. Start here before adding methylfolate. Bright yellow urine: expected and harmless.
With breakfast · Start before methylfolate
TMG (Trimethylglycine)
500–1,500mg daily
Provides an alternative methylation route (BHMT pathway) that bypasses MTHFR entirely — converting homocysteine to methionine via a different mechanism. Critical when homocysteine is not normalizing on methylfolate alone. Also supports liver Phase 2 directly.
With meals · AM and PM
Magnesium Glycinate
300–400mg elemental
Required cofactor for COMT enzyme activity. Without adequate magnesium, COMT cannot methylate catechol estrogens even when SAMe is available. The glycinate form supports GABA (Pillar 1), COMT activity (Pillar 3), and HPA axis regulation (Pillar 2). One supplement — three pillars.
Evening · Supports sleep and COMT simultaneously
P5P (Pyridoxal-5-Phosphate)
25–50mg as P5P form only · Not pyridoxine HCl
Active B6 supports the transsulfuration pathway — processing homocysteine into cysteine and glutathione. Also supports DAO enzyme activity — directly relevant to histamine intolerance. Do not exceed 100mg/day long-term.
With B2 and breakfast
Peripheral neuropathy risk at very high doses (>100mg/day)
Dietary Protocol
Diet Is Protocol in This Pillar — Not Optional Background Advice
Cruciferous Vegetables Daily
Mechanism: DIM and I3C precursors → shifts Phase 1 toward 2-OHE1
Broccoli, cauliflower, Brussels sprouts, kale, cabbage, arugula. Aim for 1–2 cups daily. Lightly steamed preferred. Add broccoli sprouts specifically — 10–100x more sulforaphane than mature broccoli. Easy to grow at home in 5 days with a mason jar and seeds. Even 1 tablespoon of sprouts provides meaningful NRF2 activation.
Ground Flaxseed Daily
Mechanism: Lignans reduce beta-glucuronidase · Directly bind estrogen in gut
Must be freshly ground — whole seeds pass undigested. Store ground flax in the freezer. 2 tablespoons per day is the clinical dose. Effect on estrogen metabolism is well-documented and measurable at 6–8 weeks. Flax oil alone does not provide this benefit — the lignan content is in the fiber portion.
Limit or Eliminate Alcohol
Mechanism: Increases CYP1B1 (drives 4-OHE1) · Inhibits Phase 2 methylation · Depletes DAO
Alcohol is the single most impactful dietary driver of unfavorable estrogen metabolism. It increases CYP1B1, inhibits Phase 2 methylation, depletes DAO (worsens histamine intolerance), and disrupts the estrobolome. For slow COMT women: any alcohol during HRT is a meaningful risk amplifier. Your DUTCH test will show the impact on metabolite ratios.
High-Fiber Diet (30–35g/day)
Mechanism: Estrobolome substrate · Microbiome diversity · Transit time reduction
Priority sources: legumes (highest fiber density), ground flaxseed, vegetables, psyllium husk, resistant starch (green banana, cooled rice or potatoes). Diversity of fiber sources = diversity of microbiome species. Eliminate ultra-processed food — emulsifiers (polysorbate 80, carrageenan) directly damage the gut mucosal layer at even low doses.
Omega-3 Fatty Acids
Mechanism: Reduces CYP1B1 activity · Supports Phase 2 enzyme production · Anti-inflammatory
Fatty fish (salmon, sardines, mackerel) 3x per week OR 2–3g EPA+DHA daily. The omega-6:omega-3 ratio should be below 4:1 — standard Western diet runs 15–20:1. Swap soybean, corn, and sunflower oils for olive oil, avocado oil, or coconut oil — these drive the inflammatory prostaglandin cascade that worsens mast cell activation and 16α-OHE1 production.

The Pillar 3 Commitment

Hormone metabolism changes are slower than the cortisol and sleep shifts of Pillars 1 and 2. Expect 8–12 weeks before metabolite ratios shift meaningfully on a repeat DUTCH panel. What you will notice sooner: reduced breast tenderness within 4–6 weeks on Calcium D-glucarate, improved GI symptoms within 3–4 weeks on the estrobolome protocol, and reduced estrogen-dominant symptoms as the gut reabsorption loop closes. The genetics are permanent. The protocol adapts to them. That’s precision medicine. That’s Rebuilt Rebels.
